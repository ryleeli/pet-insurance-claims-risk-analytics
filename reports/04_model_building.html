Pet Insurance Claim Severity Prediction¶

Objective¶

Develop predictive models to estimate expected pet insurance claim severity using customer, policy and engineered risk variables.

The objective is to compare multiple modelling approaches and identify the most suitable model for pricing and underwriting applications.

Business Question¶

Can claim severity be accurately predicted using available policyholder and pet characteristics?

Which modelling approach provides the best predictive performance for estimating expected insurer claim costs?

The Linear Regression model explains only a modest proportion of claim severity variation (R² = 0.13).

The baseline model therefore provides a useful benchmark for evaluating more advanced machine learning algorithms.

Decision Tree¶

Objective¶

Develop a Decision Tree Regression model to capture non-linear relationships between policy characteristics and claim severity.

Unlike Linear Regression, Decision Trees partition the feature space into homogeneous regions, enabling more flexible modelling of complex insurance pricing relationships.

The model serves as an intermediate benchmark before ensemble methods such as Random Forest.

Random Forest¶

Objective¶

Develop a Random Forest Regression model to improve predictive performance by combining multiple decision trees.

Random Forest reduces overfitting through bootstrap sampling and feature randomness, making it one of the most widely used algorithms for insurance pricing and claim severity prediction.

This model serves as the primary benchmark for evaluating non-linear predictive performance.

Executive Summary¶

This notebook compared four supervised machine learning models for predicting expected pet insurance claim severity.

The objective was to evaluate whether more advanced ensemble learning methods improve predictive performance over a simple Linear Regression benchmark.

Four regression models were evaluated:

  • Linear Regression
  • Decision Tree
  • Random Forest
  • Gradient Boosting Regressor

Performance was assessed using Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and R².

Among the evaluated models, Gradient Boosting achieved the best overall predictive performance, producing the lowest prediction error and the highest explanatory power.

Feature importance analysis consistently identified age-related variables as the strongest drivers of claim severity, followed by coverage design and breed risk. These findings are consistent with the earlier exploratory analysis and support the business assumption that both customer characteristics and policy structure influence expected insurance costs.

Although the overall predictive performance remains moderate, the modelling results demonstrate that non-linear ensemble methods capture claim severity patterns more effectively than traditional linear models.

The resulting model can support actuarial pricing, underwriting decision-making and customer risk segmentation by providing more accurate estimates of expected claim costs.
